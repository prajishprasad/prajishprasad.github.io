ACM ITiCSE 2023 Trip Report: Undertones of a Brave New AI World

Author

Prajish Prasad

Published

July 12, 2023

This year, ITiCSE was held in the beautiful city of Turku, Finland. This was my second ITiCSE, but the first I attended in person. It was great reconnecting with some people I met before, and I had a great time getting introduced to new people and exploring the amazing work people were doing in computing education. Not surprisingly, both the keynotes and many of the papers talked about how things are going to change with the presence of Generative AI technologies.

Day 1

The first day began with important announcements from SIGCSE board members regarding a new ACM virtual conference, and a new award on broadening participation in computing. Mikko Laakso, as the local organizing chair, in his own jolly and unimitable style, welcomed all of us to the conference and to the beautiful city of Turku.

Keynote 1 - K-12 Computing Education for the AI Era: From Data Literacy to Data Agency; Matti Tedre and Henriikka Vartiainen

The first keynote was by Matti Tedre and Henriikka Vartiainen, both from the University of Eastern Finland. Matti started the keynote by sharing details from a 1999 NRC report titled ‘Why should everyone know about computing’. The report highlighted that everyone needed to know about computing because of the tremendous opportunities in all spheres of life, such as making things easier for a person, enabling opportunities to learn, and giving oneself an edge on the types of computing jobs which would erupt in the coming years. The situation now, 25 years on, has changed. Do these reasons still hold? With the rapid advancement of AI technologies, other concerns loom large in the horizon. How should teaching and learning of computer science change in step with such rapid advancements?

The dominant paradigm of computational thinking has historically been based on fundamental characteristics such as determinism, step-wise execution, discrete program states, strict syntax and well established notional machines. However, children today are exposed to technologies like iPhones, TikTok algorithms and AI, and often wonder how these technologies work. Matti argued that the challenge for K-12 teachers is that such scenarios cannot be described using rule-based approaches. Hence, a different paradigm is needed to teach such data driven approaches. The problem solving workflow changes in the data driven approach, moving towards activities such as collecting data, and training, evaluating and refining a model. In fact, the philosophy of programming in data driven approaches transition towards a more inductive approach (much like science?), which also involves some non-determinism, parallelism and emergent behaviours. This on the other hand brings in new problems such as the opacity of such algorithms, and the lack of explainability tools. In short, Matti made compelling arguments that the ability of classical programming to solve data driven problems does not work, and also urged for a more focussed effort on doing research on how to teach data-driven computing.

The rest of the presentation focussed on several examples of how Matti and Henrikka’s group has tried to introduce AI concepts to students, and their efforts towards helping teachers effectively integrate Ai in their classrooms. The entire presentation can be found here

Programming – code quality and misconceptions

The next session I attended had 3 papers in the broad theme of programming code quality and misconceptions.

The first papers presented findings of A Systematic Mapping Study of Code Quality in Education. The authors argue that understanding code quality is particularly important at this time, with the prevalence of Generative AI. Code quality is often used as a catch all word, which are used in different contexts. In the paper, code quality refers to the analyzing and reflecting on the static characteristics of the code such as documentation, layout, naming, flow, and expressions. The authors conducted a systematic mapping study, which is similar to a literature review, but also looks at trends over time and closely related constructs. Their findings show that the most commpon aspects of code quality in literature are - code smells, design patterns, metrics, readability, and refactoring. There has been a significant emphasis on assessing student program quality using automated programming tools. However, other aspects such as introducing code quality as part of course materials and curriculum has received less importance. All the 195 papers in the systematic mapping study have been mapped into an table available online.
